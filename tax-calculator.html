Old vs New Tax Regime – What's Actually Different?

Since FY 2020-21, India has had two parallel income tax systems. The old regime has higher slab rates but allows over 70 exemptions and deductions (HRA, 80C, home loan interest, LTA, etc.). The new regime has lower, more progressive slab rates but disallows most deductions — only the standard deduction of ₹75,000 is available for salaried employees.

Starting FY 2024-25, the new regime is the default regime. If you don't explicitly choose the old regime with your employer (via Form 10IEA), you'll automatically be taxed under the new regime.

Who Benefits from the Old Regime?

The old regime is generally better if your annual deductions are substantial. As a rough rule: if your deductions exceed ₹3.75 lakh (for income around ₹10–15 lakh), the old regime typically wins. The main deductions that make the old regime worthwhile are HRA (for those paying high rent), home loan interest (up to ₹2 lakh under Section 24b), Section 80C (₹1.5 lakh), and NPS (additional ₹50,000 under 80CCD 1B).

Who Benefits from the New Regime?

The new regime suits people who don't have significant deductions — those who don't own a home, don't pay HRA, or are early in their careers with limited 80C investments. It's also simpler: no need to maintain investment proofs or submit declarations to your employer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I switch between old and new regime every year?
Salaried employees can switch between regimes every financial year at the time of filing their ITR. However, if you have business income, switching back to the old regime is allowed only once. The choice you communicate to your employer affects your monthly TDS, but the final decision is made when filing your ITR.
Is HRA exemption available in the new tax regime?
No. HRA exemption is only available under the old regime. Under the new regime, HRA is fully taxable as part of your gross salary. If you pay significant rent (especially in metro cities), this single deduction often makes the old regime more beneficial.
What is the rebate under Section 87A?
Section 87A provides a tax rebate that makes your effective tax liability zero if your taxable income is below a threshold. Under the old regime, rebate is ₹12,500 (income ≤ ₹5 lakh). Under the new regime, rebate is ₹25,000 (income ≤ ₹7 lakh). This means if your new-regime taxable income is ≤ ₹7 lakh after standard deduction, you pay zero tax.
Does the surcharge apply to me?
Surcharge applies only to high-income earners: 10% if income is ₹50L–₹1Cr, 15% if ₹1–2Cr, 25% if ₹2–5Cr (new regime), and 37% if above ₹5Cr (old regime only; new regime capped at 25%). This calculator shows basic tax without surcharge — relevant for most salaried employees.
Which regime should I choose if I'm confused?
Use this calculator with your actual numbers. If the savings difference is under ₹10,000, the new regime is often the simpler choice — no investment proofs to submit, no declarations needed. If the old regime saves significantly more (₹20,000+), it's worth the paperwork.
