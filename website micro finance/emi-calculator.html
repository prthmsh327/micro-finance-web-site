What is an EMI?

An EMI (Equated Monthly Instalment) is the fixed amount you pay your lender every month to repay your loan. Each EMI has two parts: a portion that goes toward repaying the principal (the amount you borrowed) and a portion that covers the interest charged for that month.

In the early months of a loan, a larger share of your EMI goes toward interest. As time passes, the interest portion reduces and the principal repayment portion increases. This is called amortization.

EMI Formula

The standard EMI formula used by all banks in India is:

EMI = P × r × (1 + r)^n ÷ ((1 + r)^n – 1)

Where: P = Principal loan amount | r = Monthly interest rate (Annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100) | n = Total number of monthly instalments

Example: For a ₹50,00,000 home loan at 8.5% annual interest for 20 years: r = 8.5/12/100 = 0.00708, n = 240 months. EMI ≈ ₹43,391.

Frequently Asked Questions

How can I reduce my EMI?
You can reduce your EMI in three ways: (1) Make a larger down payment to reduce the principal, (2) Negotiate a lower interest rate — even 0.5% difference can save lakhs over a 20-year loan, (3) Choose a longer tenure — but this means more total interest paid overall.
What happens if I miss an EMI?
Missing an EMI attracts a late payment penalty (typically 1–2% of the overdue amount) and negatively impacts your CIBIL score. After 3 consecutive missed EMIs, the loan is classified as an NPA (Non-Performing Asset) by the bank.
Does prepayment reduce my EMI or tenure?
Most banks give you the option to either reduce your EMI amount or keep the EMI the same and reduce the loan tenure. Reducing tenure is generally more beneficial as it results in less total interest paid.
Is the EMI amount fixed for the entire loan period?
For fixed-rate loans, yes. For floating-rate loans (linked to MCLR or repo rate), the EMI changes when the base rate changes. Most home loans in India are floating rate. Check your loan agreement for the type you have.
What is the amortization schedule?
The amortization schedule is a table showing the breakdown of each payment into principal and interest, and the remaining loan balance after each payment. It helps you understand exactly how your loan is being paid off over time.
